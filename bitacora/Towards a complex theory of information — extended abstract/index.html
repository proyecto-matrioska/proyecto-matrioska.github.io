Towards a complex theory of information — extended abstract

Publicado: 2025-10-01

This is the abstract for a talk I will give at ICPHILCOMP25.

We give the foundations for a complex theory of information. It is complex in the sense that it accounts for both qualitative and quantitative aspects of information, therefore beholding both subjective and objective informational phenomena. It is also complex in the sense that approaches a layer of abstraction structuring information as such, thus, a layer meta-structuring any kind of discourse. By its nature, abstractions made here through a schematic approach, allow simple formal and graphical representations, coming with a rich and novel top-down visual reasoning methodology grounded in a complex theory of duality. On these grounds, our theory departs from the code-theoretical and algorithmic approaches of Shannon and Weber, and Chaitin respectively, by instead adopting a phenomenological and semiotic perspective.

We started from a first observation that everything is information for us. Thus, we start from a phenomenological approach. After the pure experience of reality emerges the mental phenomena, and from there a subjective —and inter-subjective— categorization process. Information is then the result of a phenomenological reduction process synthesizing formal abstraction and subjective interpretation of the experience of any kind of reality. Such a perspective positions us in a layer of an abstract categorical framework where information resides as such and structures itself. Also, it further advances us into a new metaphysics of schematic abstraction as foundational grounds.

The link between the purity of abstraction and the pragmatics of visual reasoning resides in a complex theory of duality. Complex duality abstracts intentionality of discursive duality with a categorical approach, where we observe categories as complex discursive universes enclosing any kind of discourses —or argumentations— exhibiting qualities associated to an hypostasis or simple abstract term. Accordingly, we approach the phenomenological reduction process as a process of phenomena categorization. Furthermore, this process must be by differentiation as it ends into dual categories distinguishing a particular phenomenon from others.

This categorical standpoint provides the first informational contention schema. The categorical schema splits the universe of discourse into two by gestating a subcategory of discourses, that is, a simplification we made from the complexity of the universe. So a universe of discourse may contain discourses defining other universes, and complexity may contain its simplifications.

By pointing to those universes —like all phenomena— we are also distinguishing them from others in a transcendent universe. What we approach with this contention triad is the base for a foundational archetype: an intentional structure of information according to its own complexity qualities, in such a manner that gives a schematic meta-structure to discursive complexity. This approach not only turns categories into composable intensional entities, but also opens local windows to complexity by means of schematic intensional relations.

This schema has a geometrical equivalent simplifying contention into oppositions. As a matter of fact, we derive it from an abstract geometrical operation: we evert contention outwards as abstract oppositions, where containing universes become intensional or complex, and contained ones become extensional or simple. It should be clear that this pair of dualities are not sufficient to describe any kind of abstract quality. This is because the schematic approach abstracts the quality of opposition itself, so the more abstract a pair of dual terms are, the more they can be used as structural intension for other pairs of dual terms. Intension-extension, complex-simple, theory-practice, game-strategy, spirit-matter, etc., become, together or separately, the structural intentionality of more complex dualities.

Our third transcendent category also acquires an abstract quality that must meet its dual on the same transcendent —i.e., more complex— universe of discourse. Here, we note that the archetypal essence of our schematic approach assures that the schema applies to all information, including the schema itself. This archetypal foundation enables an abstract categorical framework by assuring that any pair of dual terms we can structure must have also a dual pair of opposite quality on the same universe of discourse. Also, this triadic arrangement is the foundation for schematic abstract dialectics emerging from opposition and quality abstraction.

If for every duality there exists —metaphysically— another one of opposite quality, then also one duality becomes intension of the other. Thus, an oppositional square emerges where every term (node) is opposite in abstract quality to its two neighbors. The transcendent oppositions also conform then a new square, more complex than the previous one. Of course, if archetypal duality schematically applies to duality itself, then also applies to these new squares, and as we can continue abstracting and synthesizing as far as we wish, this applies also to new squares and to any intensional grouping or arrangement we can come up from them by following their oppositional abstract qualities.

Fractality emerges then as a consequence of the schematic meta-structuring approach, together with a new ontology of complexity, as every binary, triadic or tetradic arrangement we make based on these oppositional abstract qualities will certainly delimit some universe of discourse of increasing complexity in relation to the abstractions we make to the oppositional structure itself. Even more, other more complex structures preserving aforementioned properties may become a pragmatical representation of more complex archetypes that, and through a graph-theoretic or semiotic approaches foresee a fruitful path of varied applications.

We present advances on the philosophical and pragmatical aspects of the theory. The methodology that emerges has been applied to the theory itself, resulting in a proper ontology of generic categories of information. Advances into akin ontology of computer programming, and others, are also presented. Future work includes the mathematical and graph-theoretic analysis of these abstract structures, and also further research on the computational-theoretic aspects of the theory.
